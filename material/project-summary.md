# Project Summary: Winter Ankle Draft Risk Model (Ankle Draft 2)

## Objective

Update the ankle draft risk model in ASHRAE 55 (Liu et al., 2017;
Schiavon et al., 2016) to better account for winter-specific conditions.
The current model was developed for summer cooling scenarios
(UFAD/displacement ventilation with bare ankles) and may overestimate
draft risk when occupants wear winter clothing, leading to unnecessary
perimeter heating and increased energy consumption.

## Experimental Design

-   **Design**: 3×3 within-subjects factorial study
-   **Factors**:
    -   Supply air temperature (SAT): 15°C, 17°C, 19°C (coded as
        low/medium/high; sessions named "yosemite", "yellowstone",
        "sequoia")
    -   Air speed at ankle level: \~0.2–0.6 m/s (low/medium/high)
-   **Participants**: \~45 adults (21–55 years), diverse demographics
-   **Clothing**: \~0.75 clo (winter office attire with covered ankles)
-   **Room conditions**: PMV ≈ −0.5 (slightly cool), \~22°C operative
    temperature, 50% RH, 1.1 met (seated)

## Protocol

-   Introduction session (1h) + 3 experimental sessions (2h each)
-   Each session: 3 × 20-min exposures to different air speed conditions
    at one SAT level, with 20-min rest periods between (called
    Adaptation), participants attend three sessions (each with specific
    SAT)
-   Custom displacement diffuser positioned \~0.7m behind participants
    directing airflow at ankles

## Measurements

-   **Subjective**: Thermal sensation, comfort, preference, and
    acceptability (overall and local—especially ankles/feet) via surveys
-   **Physiological**: Skin temperature at ankles, feet, and hands using
    iButton sensors

## Hypothesis

Increased draft air speed and decreased draft air temperature will
increase local thermal discomfort at the ankles, even when overall
thermal conditions are acceptable—but winter clothing will attenuate
this effect compared to the original summer-clothing model.
